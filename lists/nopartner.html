Camp David 2000: What Really Happened

  • The myth: 'Arafat rejected Barak's generous offer and walked away, proving there is no partner.' The reality: No formal written offer was ever presented. Israel proposed a non-contiguous Palestinian 'state' on 50‚Äì60% of the West Bank, with Israeli control of borders, airspace, water, and the Jordan Valley, and no sovereignty over East Jerusalem.
  • Robert Malley (Clinton's special assistant for Arab-Israeli affairs, 2000): 'The portrayal of Arafat as the intransigent villain and Barak as the bold peacemaker is a caricature. Barak's proposals fell short of Palestinian minimum requirements and were communicated as non-negotiable.'
  • Settlements: During the Camp David talks, Israel continued constructing settlements. In 2000 alone, 4,800 new housing units were tendered in the West Bank.

Clinton Parameters & Taba 2001

  • December 2000: President Clinton presented parameters: Palestinian state on 94‚Äì96% of West Bank, land swap of 1‚Äì3%, Arab neighborhoods of East Jerusalem under Palestinian sovereignty, Jewish neighborhoods under Israeli sovereignty, and a solution for refugees based on UNGA 194.
  • The Palestinian leadership accepted the Clinton Parameters with some reservations. Israel accepted with 14 reservations that fundamentally altered the proposal ‚Äì including maintaining control over the Jordan Valley and rejecting refugee return.
  • Taba Summit (January 2001): Israeli and Palestinian negotiators came closer than ever to a final agreement. Negotiations were terminated by Israeli Prime Minister Barak amid his impending electoral defeat.

Arab Peace Initiative (2002)

  • The Arab League unanimously adopted the Saudi-initiated Arab Peace Initiative: full normalization with Israel in exchange for full Israeli withdrawal from territories occupied in 1967, a just solution for Palestinian refugees based on UNGA 194, and a Palestinian state with East Jerusalem as its capital.
  • Israel's official response: Ignored the initiative. Prime Minister Sharon rejected it outright; subsequent governments never formally engaged with it as a basis for negotiation.
  • The initiative was re-endorsed in 2007 and 2017. Polls consistently show 60‚Äì70% of Israelis support it, yet Israeli governments have never accepted it.

Olmert Offer (2008)

Prime Minister Ehud Olmert (2008): Proposed Palestinian state on 93% of West Bank, 5.8% land swap, no sovereignty over East Jerusalem's holy basin, no right of return, and Israeli presence in Jordan Valley for 40 years. President Abbas requested clarification on maps and borders; Olmert left office without responding.
  • Abbas responded: 'I said yes immediately, but asked for the maps. Olmert never provided them. The offer disappeared when he resigned.'
  • The narrative that 'Abbas rejected a generous offer' is contradicted by Olmert himself, who later confirmed: 'We didn't reach an agreement. I take full responsibility for not completing it.'
  • Settlements: During Olmert's tenure (2006‚Äì2009), settler population increased by 30,000 ‚Äì the largest growth of any post-Oslo government.

Palestinian Statehood Bid (2011)

  • President Abbas submitted Palestine's application for full UN membership ‚Äì a non-violent diplomatic initiative for statehood within 1967 borders. The US threatened to veto, and the bid was downgraded to non-member observer state status (2012).
  • Israeli response: Accelerated settlement construction. In the month following the bid, Israel announced 3,000 new housing units in East Jerusalem and the West Bank.

Scholarly Consensus

  • Rashid Khalidi (2020): 'The "no partner" myth is the most successful propaganda trope of the Israeli peace process. It shifts blame from the occupier to the occupied, from the party with overwhelming power to the party with none.'
  • Nathan Thrall (2017): 'The claim that Israel has no partner for peace is a self-fulfilling prophecy. By expanding settlements, refusing to negotiate final status issues, and rejecting Arab peace initiatives, Israel ensures there is no partner on its own terms.'
  • Henry Siegman (2007): 'The real obstacle to peace is not the absence of a Palestinian partner, but Israel's unwillingness to end the occupation.'
